Methodology
Development of the FishParT Function
The FishParT function was developed as a custom R-based tool to estimate length-based indicators for fisheries stock assessment, integrating Monte Carlo simulation for parameter estimation and non-parametric bootstrap methods for Froese sustainability indicators (Froese et al., 2016). The function was designed to analyze length-frequency data, providing estimates of key biological parameters such as maximum length (Lmax), asymptotic length (Linf), length at maturity (Lmat), and optimum length (Lopt), alongside sustainability metrics including the percentage of mature individuals (Pmat), optimally sized individuals (Popt), and mega-spawners (Pmega). The development process was guided by the need to incorporate uncertainty and variability in parameter estimates, addressing reviewer feedback from Fisheries Research emphasizing sensitivity analysis (Thorson et al., 2013).
The function was initially built using base R and extended with packages such as graphics for plotting, stats for statistical computations, and openxlsx for exporting results to Excel. It evolved iteratively, starting with a basic framework that derived Lmax from the maximum observed length and Linf as Lmax / 0.95, a common approximation in fisheries science (Froese and Binohlan, 2000). Subsequent enhancements allowed users to provide fixed values for Linf and Lmat, with optional standard deviation parameters (Linf_sd and Lmat_sd) to introduce controlled random variation, reflecting uncertainties in these parameters as sourced from databases like FishBase or empirical studies. The inclusion of these parameters was a direct response to the need to explore sensitivity in parameter estimates, ensuring the model could simulate realistic variability in growth and maturity metrics.
Data input requirements were standardized to a two-column data frame (Length and Frequency), with validation checks to ensure positive frequencies, sufficient sample size (minimum two rows), and adequate length class diversity (minimum three unique lengths). Warnings were implemented for datasets with skewed frequency distributions (e.g., >80% in a single length class) to alert users of potential biases in variability estimates.
Monte Carlo Simulation for Parameter Estimation
Monte Carlo simulation was employed to generate probabilistic distributions of length-based parameters, accounting for inherent variability in biological data. The function uses a user-defined number of resamples (default set to 1000) to create simulated datasets. The process begins with the generation of Lmax_samples, which, when Linf is not provided, are drawn from a normal distribution centered on the maximum observed length (Lmax) with a standard deviation of 1 cm, constrained between 90% and 110% of Lmax to reflect biological plausibility. Linf_samples are then derived as Lmax_samples / 0.95, aligning with the asymptotic length approximation.
When a user-specified Linf is provided, Linf_samples are generated by adding random noise from a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation defined by Linf_sd (default 0.5 cm) to the fixed Linf value, repeated across the resample size. Corresponding Lmax_samples are calculated as Linf_samples / 0.95, ensuring consistency between the two parameters. Similarly, if Lmat is provided, Lmat_samples are created by adding noise from a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of Lmat_sd (default 0.5 cm). In the absence of a provided Lmat, Lmat_samples are estimated using the empirical relationship Lmat = 10^(0.8979 * log10(Linf) - 0.0782) with added random noise (standard deviation 0.015), as adapted from fisheries growth models (Froese and Binohlan, 2000).
Optimum length (Lopt_samples) is calculated as 10^(1.053 * log10(Lmat_samples) - 0.0565) with a standard deviation of 0.015, and its 10% bounds (Lopt_p10_samples and Lopt_m10_samples) are derived by adding or subtracting 10% of Lopt_samples. These samples are combined into a matrix (parameter_estimates) for further analysis, enabling the propagation of uncertainty through the model.
Estimation of Confidence Intervals
Confidence intervals (CIs) for length parameters (Lmax, Linf, Lmat, Lopt, Lopt_p10, Lopt_m10) and Froese indicators (Pmat, Popt, Pmega) were estimated using the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of their respective sample distributions, corresponding to a 95% confidence level. For length parameters, CIs were derived directly from the parameter_estimates matrix generated by the Monte Carlo simulation. The mean of each parameter across resamples was calculated using the apply function with the mean method, while the lower and upper bounds were obtained using the quantile function with probabilities of 0.025 and 0.975, respectively.
For Froese indicators, a non-parametric bootstrap approach was implemented. The original length-frequency data were expanded into individual observations based on their frequencies, and resamples were drawn with replacement to match the total frequency sum. For each bootstrap iteration, the proportions of mature (Pmat), optimally sized (Popt), and mega-spawner (Pmega) individuals were calculated as percentages of the total frequency within defined length ranges: mature individuals between Lmat and Lmax, optimally sized between Lopt_m10 and Lopt_p10, and mega-spawners between Lopt_p10 and Lmax. These proportions were constrained between 0% and 100% to ensure biological realism. CIs for these indicators were similarly computed using the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles from the froese_indicators matrix, with adjustments to cap lower bounds at 0% and upper bounds at 100%.
Non-Parametric Bootstrap for Froese Indicators
The non-parametric bootstrap method was applied to estimate the variability of Froese sustainability indicators, complementing the Monte Carlo approach for length parameters. For each of the 1000 resamples, a bootstrap sample was generated by randomly selecting observations with replacement from the expanded dataset, preserving the original frequency distribution. The length ranges for each indicator were defined using the corresponding parameter estimates from the Monte Carlo simulation for that iteration. The sums of frequencies within these ranges (sum_mat, sum_opt, sum_mega) were calculated and divided by the total frequency (sumT) to derive the percentage indicators. This process was repeated across all resamples, producing a distribution of indicator values from which CIs were extracted.
Visualization and Output
The function generates a suite of visualizations to aid interpretation, including histograms, density plots, boxplots, and barplots, saved as PDF files (e.g., "Froese_Indicators_Histograms.pdf"). These plots display the distributions of length parameters and Froese indicators, with mean values marked by red lines and CI bounds by dashed black lines. A length-frequency plot with a smoothed loess curve and a target vs. catch comparison barplot are also produced. All numerical results, including mean estimates, CIs, and target vs. catch comparisons, are exported to an Excel file ("FishPar_Outputs.xlsx") for further analysis.
Overall Approach
The FishParT function integrates Monte Carlo simulation and non-parametric bootstrap methods to provide a robust framework for assessing fisheries stock status based on length-frequency data. By allowing user-defined inputs for Linf and Lmat with customizable variability (via Linf_sd and Lmat_sd), the function facilitates sensitivity analysis, addressing potential uncertainties in parameter estimates. The combination of probabilistic parameter estimation and bootstrap-derived indicators ensures a comprehensive evaluation of stock health, aligning with best practices in fisheries research (Froese et al., 2016; Thorson et al., 2013). This methodology was validated through iterative testing with synthetic and real datasets, ensuring reliability for publication and practical application.
